# Quotas
Rather than directly imposing a price increase like a tariff does by adding a tax on imports, a quota limits the quantity which may be imported. But ultimately the effect is **nearly** the same in terms of the graph. The limited supply of cheaper foreign goods drives prices up. Quotas are regulated by licensing to either foreign suppliers or domestic importers

**The remainder of this note will look very similar to [[Tariff Graph]]. Key differences will be emphasized with ALL CAPS**
# The Areas/Shapes
## Without the tariff
When the price is at P<sup>W</sup>, shapes A, B, C, and D are all part of [[Consumer Surplus]].
## With the tariff
When the price is at P<sub>quota</sub>:
- **A:** [[Producer Surplus]]
	- domestic producers regain the ability to sell to individuals who are willing to purchase rice at this higher price
- **B:** [[#Deadweight loss]]
- **C:** DEPENDS ON IMPLEMENTATION OF LICENSING
	- If **domestic importers** get the licensing for foreign goods they gain C
	- If **foreign suppliers** get the licensing they gain C
- **D:** [[#Deadweight loss]]
## Deadweight loss
*He emphasized this a lot in lectures*
When a quota is imposed the price shifts from P<sup>W</sup> to P<sub>quota</sub>. In the process, areas B and D are lost and not gained by anyone. Hence why they are called deadweight loss Without the tariff this is part of [[Consumer Surplus]].
