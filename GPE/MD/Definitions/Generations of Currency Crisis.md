Economics scholarship classifies currency crises into 3 generations
# First Generations
A government with a currency peg is spending more than it earns (budget deficit) and prints money to cover this deficit. Attempting to account for the ensuing inflation begins to drain the central bank's foreign reserves until a collapse seems inevitable and investors engage in a [[Speculative Attack]].
> [!example]- Example
> Mexico consistently had large budget deficits in the 70s and 80s. To fund this deficit, they expanded the domestic money supply. Because they were trying to keep their exchange rate fixed to the U.S. Dollar, they bought up a lot of their currency on forex markets to try to maintain the exchange rate. As investors saw foreign reserves running out, they launched a speculative attack which served as the tipping point that forced the government to devalue.
# Second Generation
Investors believe that the government will not maintain a currency peg as it becomes increasingly politically controversial to do so. If they predict this as coming they will launch a [[Speculative Attack]] and this will serve as a self fulfilling prophecy of sorts as this makes it even more difficult to maintain a currency peg.
> [!example]- Example
> The UK was a part of the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM) which pegged the Pound to the German Mark. As Germany raised inflation rates, the UK had to follow suit even though they were in a deep recession and faced rising unemployment. Investors (Jeff loves to name George Soros) bet that the British government would not hold on to the peg as their population suffered. With this expectation they sold billions of pounds in what came to be called [[Black Wednesday (1992)]]. This worsened the situation and led to the UK abandoning the ERM peg the same day. 
# Third Generation
Private banks borrow foreign currencies (USD for example) but earn revenue in their domestic currency. If the value of their domestic currency goes down even just a little compared to the USD, their debt quickly becomes almost impossible to pay.
> [!example]- Example
> Thai banks in the 90s borrowed massive amounts of USD because American interest rates were low, they felt secure in the Thai currency peg to the dollar. But when the peg was abandoned in response to a real estate bubble burst, banks and other firms who had borrowed USD suddenly needed way more of the Thai currency than expected to pay back the same amount of USD.
