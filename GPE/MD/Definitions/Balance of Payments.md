The balance of payments (BOP) is effectively an economies accounting book. Put simply, it is the record of every economic transaction that crosses a countries borders.

> [!warning]+ What BOP is NOT
> The balance of payments is NOT a measure of how much money a government has. While the balance of payments may have effects on a countries treasury, it is not the same. To make the difference clear:
> 
> **The state** (i.e. the government, the administration, the ministry of finance/department of the treasury) has what is effectively the state's own bank account.
> 
> **The economy** (i.e. the sum of every actor within a state's borders like individuals, firms, and the state itself). The BOP is the flow of capital in and out of **the economy**.

# The Accounting
As the word "balance" suggests, the BOP can be represented by an equation that should ideally result in 0:
$$
Current\ Account + Financial\ Account + Capital\ Account = 0
$$
## The Current Account
The current account tracks the flow of goods, services, and income in the economy. It can be divided into 4 components:
- **Balance of Trade**: the amount of physical goods imported subtracted from the amount of physical goods exported (exports-imports of goods)
- **Services:** trade like tourism, banking, insurance, and transport.
- **Primary income:** Earning on investments. Importantly it does not matter where these investments are held. An American owning stock in a German company still receives their dividend in the US so it is recorded here.
- **Secondary income:** Single transfer events like receiving money from an individual overseas (often in the form remittances: economic migrants sending money home which makes up a significant portions of some economies current accounts)

> [!tldr]+ The Rule
> If an economy has a positive Current Account they are a net lender, meaning they save more money than they spend (and by extension loan it out). If their current account is negative they are a net borrower, they spend more than they have and must borrow.
## The Financial Account 
The financial account tracks the "paper economy", meaning the transfer of certain financial assets. It is concerned with what you own. It is similar to the way the value of your home or an expensive piece of jewelry is a part of your net worth despite not being cash you can spend. The assets it tracks may take the form of:
- **Foreign Direct Investment (FDI):** Owning a significant (>10%) share of a foreign company, or physical capital like a factory in a foreign country.
- **Portfolio Investment:** Buying stocks (<10% of the company), bonds, crypto currency. Note that this is **not the same as primary income** in the current account because there we are concerned with what is returned (the cash made) from the asset. Here it is the ownership of that asset, the value of what is owned.
- **Other Investments:** Bank loans, currency deposits, etc. Loans to other countries are **assets**, an actor in your economy owns that debt. Loans to an actor in your economy from another economy is a **liability**. You owe them the money borrowed. Currency deposits act the same way. If a British company has a deposit in a German Bank, that German bank has a liability to that company to give it that money upon request and vice versa. 
- **Reserve Assets:** The Central Bank's holdings of assets like gold and foreign currencies (also in a few cases crypto but don't worry about that). These reserves are used to defend a currency peg or otherwise steer your currencies value on foreign exchange (forex) markets.
## Capital Account
The capital account is super minor and usually very small in modern accounting. It covers the transfer of patents, copyrights, or debt forgiveness.

# The Relationship Between Current and Financial Accounts
The Current Account and the Financial Account are mirrors of each other. Meaning that if one is negative (a deficit) the other must be positive (a surplus).
- **Current Account Deficit:** This means you are buying more goods and services from abroad than you are selling to other countries. Obviously this raises the question "How do you pay for that?". The answer is that you must sell assets such as a piece of your company (stocks/FDI), government debt (bonds), or land to foreign holders. This results in a Financial Account Surplus, an inflow of money to your economy. This money is then used to import the goods and services.
- **Current Account Surplus:** This means you are selling more goods and services than you buy meaning that you end up with extra liquid capital (cash). This is then used to buy foreign stocks, factories, or government debt, meaning a Financial Account Deficit.
