International Finance is at it's heart about the exchange of assets across borders. These equations are used to keep track of this flow, aka the practice of accounting.

> [!info]+ The Key
> S<sub>H</sub> = Household savings
> S<sub>G</sub> = Government savings
> S<sub>F</sub> = Foreign savings
> I = Investment in domestic firms
> E = Exports
> Z = Imports
# National Savings and Investment Equation

$$ I = (S_H + S_G) + S_F $$
In effect this is saying that the investment into your economies firms/companies (I), is equal to the amount of domestic savings (S<sub>H</sub> + S<sub>G</sub>, because the government and households are both domestic actors), in addition to foreign savings S<sub>F</sub>.

This equation can then of course be transformed to determine another variable. If you wanted to determine how much foreign wealth was saved in your country you could solve the following.
$$ I - (S_H + S_G) = S_F$$
This equation is subtracting domestic savings (S<sub>H</sub> + S<sub>G</sub>) from domestic investment (I), leaving us with the foreign savings (S<sub>F</sub>).
# Current Account Balance Equation
*Read more about current accounts in the note on [[Balance of Payments]]*
$$E + S_F = Z$$
This equation is adding the economies exports (E) to the foreign savings (S<sub>F</sub>) to determine how much is imported (Z). This is because when you import more than you export, those extra exports are paid for by foreign savings/investments. Naturally you can also find uses for transforming this one such as determining foreign savings using your imports and exports like so.
$$S_F = Z - E$$
With Z - E representing the trade deficit, it could also represent a surplus if exports (E) is greater than imports (Z).
